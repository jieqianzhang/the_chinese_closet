On the Struggle

The Chinese Closet and HIV

By Fan Fei, Jieqian Zhang and Tailai Zhou

What does China look like to you? For many people, China is Beijing and Shanghai, a country with modern cities, huge skyscrapers, and the world’s second largest economy. For other people, China is crowded with an enormous population, supplying a cheap labor that makes it possible for their iPhones to ship on time. But underneath this modern looking, China is lagging behind in many of its culture values. One is the country’s acceptance of homosexuality.

China has a population of 1.37 billion. Experts say there are more than 20 million gay men in the country. To put it into perspective, California’s population is around 40 million currently. Being gay is hard in China. Homosexuality was a crime there until 1997, and was considered as a mental illness until 2001. Gay marriage has never been legal. Although the Chinese society is starting to become more tolerant of the LGBT community, being gay is still stigmatized in much of China today.

The issue becomes more complicated when HIV is involved. The disease is on the rise in China’s gay community. According to the Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), HIV positive rate among gay men in China is about 8 percent in 2015. That’s up from 6 percent four years ago, in 2011. According to the Chinese CDC, gay men now account for 27 percent of all new HIV infections. And yet experts say gay men make up less than five percent of China’s population.

There are many reasons for the rise of HIV in the gay community. Wang Xiaodong, the founder of Chengdu Tongle, a nonprofit dedicated to HIV prevention in China, said that gay men are much more likely to contract HIV than the general population. “For the general population, the HIV rate might be point zero one percent. It’s point one percent in some places. But for the gay community, HIV positive rate might be as high as 10 percent. The difference is huge,” He said. “Also without any protection, gay sex is much more likely to transmit HIV than straight sex.”

Dr. Liao Meizhen, who works for Shandong Center for Disease Control and Prevention in China said not enough people are using condoms in the gay community.

“In Shandong Province, the rate of condom use in the gay community is only in the range of 40 to 50 percent in the latest month ,” Liao said. “I interviewed many gay men about their use of condoms. They prefer pleasure over safety. The reason could be that there’s too much oppression on gay sex, and then they feel condom prevents them from getting the pleasure they want. Especially those gay men in heterosexual marriages, they have experienced long term pressure not to have gay sex. And when they do have it, they tend to engage in high risk sex.”

Liao also said the CDC has limited resources to contain HIV. “Shandong Province has over 100 counties, and each county has a population over one million people. The least populated county still has three hundred thousand to half a million people. But each county has only one to three CDC staff dedicated to HIV prevention.”

The lack of sex education also contributes to the rise of HIV, especially in gay youth. “It’s a Chinese tradition that we don’t talk about sex,” Zhang Beichuan, a professor at Qingdao University's Medical School said. Zhang has been researching gay men and HIV since 1990s.

“Did Sun Yat-sen talk about sex? Did Chiang Kai-shek talk about sex? Did Mao Zedong talk about sex? Deng Xiaoping didn’t talk about sex. Xi Jinping didn’t talk about sex. It’s not about party politics. It’s a heritage of anti-sex culture,” Zhang said, describing a long history of Chinese leaders who never addressed such taboo subjects.

Not talking about sex doesn’t mean the youth are not having sex. According to a survey conducted in 2009, commissioned by the United Nations Population Fund, among the young people in China who are sexually active and are aged from 15 to 24, half of them didn’t use any birth control options during their first sex.

According to the Chinese CDC, the number of HIV positive high school and college students increased sharply by 35 percent every year from 2011 to 2015. And HIV hit gay students much harder than straight students. The Chinese CDC reports that from 2014 to October 2015, gay students accounted for more than 80 percent of new student HIV infections

Zhang Beichuan, a professor at Qingdao University's Medical School, estimates that China has twenty million gay men, and about eighty percent of them are married to or will marry women.
